# Outline: Reading the Water

**Format:** Feature Essay
**Discipline:** Fly Fishing
**Core lesson:** The information you need is always present — learning to read it is the skill.

**Opening hook:**
The fish had been rising for twenty minutes before I understood what I was looking at. Not the fish — the water.

**Three-Act Structure:**

### Act I — The Discipline
- Introduce the concept of "reading water" as a fly fisher's core skill
- Explain what it actually means: current seams, depth, temperature, structure, feeding lanes
- Teach the vocabulary: seam, riffle, pool, run, eddy — briefly, naturally woven in
- The fish are almost always where they should be. You either know how to see it or you don't.
- A specific scene: standing in a river, watching the surface, learning to decode what's there

### Act II — The Drift
- The skill is transferable but the piece doesn't say so yet — it just starts doing it
- Reading water is pattern recognition built from hours of being wrong
- You learn to see not what you want to see, but what's actually there
- The surface of the river is a text. Most people see water. A reader sees information.
- The transition: "After enough time on the water, you start doing it everywhere."

### Act III — The Landing
- A quiet ending. The writer is somewhere — maybe not on a river — and notices themselves reading the surface of something
- Does not explain it. Does not say "just like in life..."
- Ends on an image or a moment that the reader completes themselves

**What NOT to say:**
"Reading water is just like reading people / a room / a market." Never say the parallel. Let the reader find it.

**Target length:** 700–900 words
