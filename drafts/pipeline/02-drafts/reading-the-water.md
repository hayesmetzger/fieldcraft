A river never looks the same twice. The water that was here a moment ago is already downstream, replaced by something new — and yet the river itself follows patterns so consistent that a skilled angler can read them like a language.

You learn to see where the fast water meets the slow. Where a submerged rock deflects the current into a seam. Where depth and shade and temperature conspire to make one particular pool more alive than everything around it. You learn this not from a book, but from hours of watching. From being wrong. From standing in cold water until the patterns reveal themselves.

The fish are almost always where they should be. The question is whether you've learned to see it yet.
