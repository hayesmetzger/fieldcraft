The hardest thing to learn in fly fishing isn't the cast. It's what happens after.

Once your fly lands on the water, your job is to let it behave as if your line doesn't exist — as if it's just a natural piece of the river, drifting exactly as the current dictates. The moment your line pulls on it, creating what anglers call "drag," the illusion breaks. The fish sees it. They almost always do.

The paradox is that achieving a drag-free drift requires enormous effort and attention — you're constantly mending the line, managing slack, reading the current — but all of that work has to be invisible in the result. The presentation has to look like you did nothing at all.

It's the same principle behind every craft that looks easy when done well.
