# Fieldcraft Writing Guide

Every piece is a practitioner telling a story to someone who's never done what they do. Not a lecture. Not a how-to. A story — the kind a grandfather tells a grandson, unhurried, specific, where the lesson arrives on its own at the end without being named.

---

## Voice

The tone splits the difference between educational editorial and storytelling narrative. Think *Outside* magazine meets a really good campfire story. The piece teaches you something real, and it does it through the momentum of a story being told.

**First person. Always.** The writer is the expert. They earned this knowledge the hard way.

**Narrative drives the education.** The reader learns about the discipline not through explanation but through experience — following the writer through a moment, a decision, a mistake, a discovery. The technical details land inside the story, not alongside it.

**Witty but dry.** Humor lives in understatement. A wry aside in the middle of a technical paragraph. Never a punchline.

**Teach, don't define.** When a term or technique needs context, weave it in naturally — a sentence, maybe two. Enough for an outsider to follow. Not a glossary entry.

**The ending is a parable, not a conclusion.** It lands with weight. It doesn't explain itself. It ends on an image, a moment, a question — and lets the reader finish it. The reader should feel like they figured it out, not like they were told.

---

## What to Avoid

- Naming the lesson. If the piece is about how reading water is like reading people, never say that. Trust the reader.
- Moralizing. The story carries the lesson.
- Summarizing at the end. If the piece worked, it doesn't need a bow.
- Opening with a thesis or a dictionary definition.
- Sounding like a magazine. Sound like a person.

---

## Length

**250–1,000 words.** One sitting. Respect the reader's time.

| Format | Length |
|--------|--------|
| Feature Essay | 600–1,000 words |
| Field Note | 250–500 words |
| Cross-Reference | 500–900 words |
| Weekend Read | 700–1,000 words |

---

## Briefing a Writer (or Sonnet)

Include:
1. The outline
2. This guide
3. Any discipline-specific research or details
4. The instruction: **Write like a grandfather telling his grandson a story. Don't name the lesson. Trust the reader.**
