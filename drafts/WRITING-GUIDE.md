# Fieldcraft Writing Guide
*For use when briefing Sonnet or any writer on a Fieldcraft piece.*

---

## What Fieldcraft Is

A platform for transferable wisdom. Every piece is rooted in a specific discipline — the smell of the river, the weight of the chisel, the sound of the engine — and earns its broader lesson through that specificity. The craft is never a metaphor. It's the real subject. The parallel emerges on its own.

---

## Voice

**Discipline and practical application come first.**
The reader should feel like they're learning something real about fly fishing, or surgery, or bread baking. The cross-disciplinary insight is the reward for paying attention — not the premise.

**Polished but not sterile.**
Think: a well-edited magazine essay with the warmth of someone telling a story around a fire. The writing is considered, but it sounds like a person wrote it.

**Specific over general.**
"The midge was a size 22 — smaller than a fingernail" beats "a very small fly." The specific detail is what makes the reader believe you.

**Moments of whimsy and humor are welcome.**
Earned lightness, not forced jokes. If something is genuinely funny, let it be funny. Don't deflate it. Don't oversell it either.

**Never lectures.**
The writer is a practitioner sharing what they know, not a teacher explaining what you should think. Show, don't preach.

---

## Structure

### Opening
Start in the middle of something. A specific moment, a specific place, a specific problem. Not "fly fishing teaches us a lot about life" — that's the last sentence of a bad essay. Instead: "The fish had been rising for twenty minutes before I figured out what they were eating."

### Body
Stay in the discipline. Go deep on the craft. Explain the actual technique, the actual challenge, the actual stakes. Trust that readers who don't know the discipline will follow if the writing is specific and alive. Use the real vocabulary.

### Conclusion
This is the parable moment — but nonfiction. The ending should land with weight, like the last line of a short story. It earns its resonance by being earned, not declared.

**The conclusion should:**
- Drive home a message without stating it
- Invite the reader to interpret it for themselves
- Not be obvious — the reader should feel like they arrived there, not like they were led
- Resist the urge to summarize or explain what just happened

The best endings leave something unresolved on purpose. A question. An image. A silence that the reader fills in.

---

## What to Avoid

- **Don't explain the parallel.** If the piece is about how reading water is like reading a market, don't say that. Let the reader see it.
- **Don't moralize.** The lesson is in the story. Trust it.
- **Don't open with a dictionary definition.** Ever.
- **Don't end with "And that's what [discipline] taught me about life."** This is the failure mode. If the piece is good, you don't need that sentence.
- **Don't hedge.** Write with confidence. If you're not sure about a detail, find out or cut it.

---

## Format Guidelines

**Target length: 250–1,000 words across all formats.** That's roughly 1–4 pages in a typical book. Fieldcraft pieces are meant to be read in one sitting — on a lunch break, between tasks, before bed. Respect the reader's time.

| Format | Length | Cadence |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Feature Essay | 600–1,000 words | 2x/week |
| Field Note | 250–500 words | 2x/week |
| Cross-Reference | 500–900 words | 1x/week |
| Interview | 500–900 words | 1x/week |
| Weekend Read | 700–1,000 words | 1x/week |

**Field Notes** are punchy. One lesson, one craft, one clean landing. No meandering.

**Feature Essays** earn their length. Every paragraph earns its place. If a section could be cut without losing anything, cut it.

**Cross-References** require two practitioners, two voices, one theme. The magic is in the contrast — where the disciplines diverge is often more interesting than where they converge.

## The Outline Format

Before any piece goes to draft, create an outline in `drafts/01-outlines/slug.md`:

```
## [Working Title]

**Format:** Feature Essay / Field Note / Cross-Reference
**Discipline(s):** 
**Core lesson (one sentence):**

**Opening hook:**
(How does the piece start? What's the scene, moment, or image?)

**Structure:**
1. 
2. 
3. 
4. 
5. 

**The ending (without spelling it out):**
(What's the last image, moment, or question? What does the reader walk away sitting with?)

**What NOT to say:**
(The obvious reading — what the piece should resist explaining)

**Target length:**
```

## Briefing Sonnet

When handing off to Sonnet for a draft, include:
1. The completed outline
2. This writing guide (or a summary of the key principles)
3. Any specific details Hayes has provided about the discipline
4. The instruction: *"Do not explain the parallel. Trust the reader."*
